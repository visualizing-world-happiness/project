What Makes Us Happy?

"Happiness" means different things to different people, but we all seem to be chasing it. While 60% of our happiness may be outside of our immediate control, the other 40% is up to us and our intential activities. Our environment significantly affects the life we live; however, people from certain countries appear to be more successful in their pursuits of happiness.

As we explore what makes these countries happier, think about what "happiness" means for you.

How is Happiness Measured?


The most recognized measure of happiness is from the World Happiness Report, a landmark survey of global happiness that ranks 156 countries by how happy their citizens perceive themselves to be. It is published annually on International Day of Happiness, March 20.

The respondents assess their lives on a scale of 0-10 (10 being the "best possible life"). The following 6 factors influence each country's overall happiness score – economic production (GPD per capita), social support (family), life expectancy (health), freedom (freedom to make life choices), absence of government corruption, and generosity (giving to others).

Economy

The economy score measures the extent to which a country's GDP contributes to calculating the Happiness Score.

We can observe that the happiest countries in the world, like Luxemburg, Singapore, and Norway, have the highest GDP per capita--resulting in a higher Economy score. We can see that there appears to be a strong correlation between Economy and Happiness scores. Therefore, having a healthy economy leads to happier lives.



After exploring the visualizations, you should have noticed clear unsurprising patterns that the happiest countries in the world score consistently high in the Freedom, Social, Economy, Health, and Generosity categories. However, even the world's happiest countries did not score exceptionally well in the Government Corruption category. Governments should be worried that the 'absence of corruption' had abysmal scores across the globe. Citizens need to believe that the government has their best interests at heart. The lowest-scoring countries in the Happiness Rankings all share disproportionately low Freedom, Economy, and Health scores, and the government of these countries needs to continue investing in these areas to help increase the happiness of its population.

However, as we have seen, if governments around the world invest in improving the factors that lead to happiness, overall happiness can see significant improvements over relatively short periods of time.
